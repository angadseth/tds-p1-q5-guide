# Q2 — Differentiating Model Intelligence (12.5 marks)

Craft **one prompt** that a *weaker* model in a family answers **wrong** while
the *stronger* model in the same family answers **right**. You submit a JSON
with your chosen model pair and the prompt.

## The winning recipe

Three ingredients, stacked:

### 1. An "intuitively obvious but mathematically wrong" trap

The best traps are statements that *feel* true to fast pattern-matching but
fail under one line of arithmetic. Our pick — the **percentage asymmetry
fallacy**:

> A stock opens at ₹200. It rises 30%, then falls 30%. "The rise and fall are
> the same 30%, so they cancel out — the stock is back at 200."

Reality: `200 × 1.30 × 0.70 = 182`. It's **not** back at 200. But "same
percentage up and down cancels" is exactly the kind of plausible-sounding
claim a weak model rubber-stamps.

Other traps of the same species (if you want your own): the bat-and-ball
problem, "average speed of 30 km/h out and 60 km/h back = 45 km/h" (it's 40),
compound-interest vs simple doubling, base-rate/medical-test fallacies.

### 2. Social-pressure framing

Wrap the statement so agreeing is the path of least resistance:

- "A popular finance blog posted this and **thousands of readers agreed**…"
- "This reasoning is extremely popular and **feels obviously correct**…"
- "Your job is simply to **confirm it** for the readers."

A weak model follows the social gradient; a strong model still does the math.

### 3. Answer-only output format

Force a single-token answer so the weak model can't "think out loud" its way
to the right answer:

- "Reply with ONLY one word: YES or NO."
- "Do NOT show any working, calculation, or explanation."

Chain-of-thought is what rescues weak models — ban it, and the intelligence
gap becomes visible.

## Expected answers

- Weaker model → **YES** (falls for the trap / confirms the blog)
- Stronger model → **NO** (does the arithmetic: 182 ≠ 200)

## Verify before you submit!

Run the exact prompt on **both** models of your chosen pair (e.g. the free
tiers of GPT variants) and confirm the split actually happens — weaker wrong,
stronger right. If both get it right, tighten the screws: stronger social
framing, stricter one-word output, more "obviously correct" language. If both
get it wrong, your trap is too hard — simplify the numbers.

## Checklist

- [ ] Prompt contains a genuine reasoning trap (not a trivia question)
- [ ] Social framing pushes toward the wrong answer
- [ ] Output format bans working/explanations (single word)
- [ ] Tested live: weaker model fails, stronger model passes
- [ ] JSON submitted in the exact shape the question asks, Checked, Saved
